% 1. Abstract
\begin{abstract}
Vision Transformer (ViT) is the backbone behind Samsung's Vision Intelligence. However, depending on the specific task, there is a growing need to enhance their efficiency. Existing efficiency paradigms either require training multiple discrete models, or increases memory footprint (e.g. Mixture-of-Experts), or relying on static elastic structures that enforce a uniform compute budget across all inputs. This approach is inherently flawed; on capable infrastructure, these models will default to the largest network size regardless of the input, wasting significant computational resources on simple images. We introduce GEAR-ViT, an entropy-guided subnetwork selection mechanism that dynamically routes inputs based on image complexity rather than a rigid budget. By utilizing the mean entropy of image patches as a proxy for complexity, GEAR-ViT routes simple images to lightweight subnetworks and complex images to higher-capacity subnetworks. Our two-phase training approach yields a vast architectural search space of $(4 \times 4)^{12}$ capacities without requiring multiple training cycles. Extensive evaluations on multiple classification benchmarks (ImageNet-1k, CIFAR10 \& 100, CUB-200, Oxford-IIIT Pets, COCO), and retrieval benchmark (COCO) demonstrate that GEAR-ViT reduces compute cost by up to 26.61\% while maintaining accuracy equivalent to full-capacity model. This work significantly advances Samsung's capability to deploy latency-sensitive vision models directly on mobile hardware, and can create positive impact for solutions like Gallery Search, Image Tagger, Landmark Detection and beyond.
\end{abstract}

% 2. Introduction
\section{Introduction}

The deployment of large-scale Vision Transformers (ViTs) \cite{dosovitskiy2020image} in real-world applications is often bottlenecked by strict memory footprint, compute constraints, and power consumption. Modern mobile and edge devices require intelligent vision capabilities to drive features such as on-device gallery search, real-time image tagging, and augmented reality. However, allocating massive compute resources uniformly across all inference requests creates a severe bottleneck. Mobile processors, such as high-end Adreno GPUs or dedicated Neural Processing Units (NPUs), are highly susceptible to thermal throttling and battery drain when subjected to sustained, maximum-capacity transformer workloads. 

Training independent models of varying capacities necessitates multiple training pipelines and results in an unacceptably high memory footprint to store the distinct weights on consumer devices. Currently, the field has attempted to address this compute-performance tradeoff through several paradigms. Mixture-of-Experts \cite{jacobs1991adaptive} (MoE) architectures allow for dynamic capacity but drastically inflate the total parameter count, often by a factor of 4x to 8x. Furthermore, MoE has limited compatibility with mobile devices; the requirement for distinct networks for each subsequent token results in substantial memory paging, load, and unload overhead, effectively stalling real-time performance. On the other hand, elastic networks (e.g., MatFormer \cite{kudugunta2024matformer}) offer a nested, weight-shared structure that elegantly reduces memory footprint. However, once a compute budget is selected globally, all images—regardless of their inherent difficulty—are processed by the exact same subnetwork, which leads to massive compute waste. 

A visually simple image does not require the same representational power as a highly complex scene. For example, classifying an image of a clear sky or a perfectly centered, isolated object requires far less computation than parsing a dense, multi-object urban landscape with heavy occlusions. If we analyze the distribution of image complexities in widely used benchmarks such as ImageNet or COCO, it closely follows a Gaussian Distribution, indicating that the vast majority of images do not fall into the "highly difficult" category. Based on these statistics, we argue that model capacity should not be statically defined; rather, it should be dynamically assigned according to the input complexity at runtime. 

To this end, we propose GEAR-ViT (Guided Entropy Architectural Routing for ViT). By using the mean entropy of raw image patches as a highly efficient proxy for image complexity, GEAR-ViT dynamically routes inputs to the smallest capable subnetwork. Instead of enforcing a rigid, predefined compute budget, our approach actively adapts the network topology—specifically the Multi-Head Attention (MHA) heads and the Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) expansion ratio—based on the intrinsic difficulty of the image. 

The routing mechanism is designed to be extremely lightweight, utilizing a 2-layer multi-layer perceptron that operates on the initial pixel-space entropy before any heavy matrix multiplications occur. On commercial mobile hardware, this dynamic allocation accelerates image processing and drastically reduces compute waste. Based on our profiling, this approach can potentially improve inference speed by up to 35\%, and reduce power consumption by up to 26\%, all while fitting within the storage footprint of a single, standard ViT-Base model.

Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Dynamic, Complexity-Aware Inference:} We introduce a novel dynamic routing mechanism that allocates compute based on image patch entropy, reducing FLOPs, latency, and power consumption by efficiently processing simple images through smaller subnetworks.
    \item \textbf{Extreme Memory Efficiency via Weight Sharing:} We develop an elastic architecture that generates $(4 \times 4)^{12}$ model capacity choices from a single weight-shared ViT-base model, entirely eliminating the need for training and storing multiple independent models on edge devices.
    \item \textbf{Lossless Latency Reduction:} We demonstrate a two-phase training paradigm utilizing a Gumbel-Softmax router that achieves up to a 26.61\% reduction in compute cost without any significant accuracy degradation across diverse classification, retrieval, and dense prediction benchmarks.
\end{itemize}

% 3. Related Work/Background
\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Vision Transformers and Static Efficiency}
Since the introduction of the Vision Transformer (ViT) \cite{dosovitskiy2020image}, transformer architectures have achieved state-of-the-art results across numerous vision tasks, rivaling and often surpassing traditional Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). However, the quadratic complexity of the self-attention mechanism with respect to the number of tokens makes ViTs inherently challenging to deploy on resource-constrained edge devices. 

To alleviate this, numerous static architectural optimizations have been proposed. MobileViT merges the localized inductive bias and feature extraction of CNNs with the global context modeling of ViTs to create architectures tailored for mobile NPUs. EfficientViT introduces hardware-aware optimizations, such as linear attention mechanisms and memory-efficient sandwich layouts, to reduce latency on edge GPUs. Swin Transformer introduced hierarchical, shifted-window attention to constrain the quadratic complexity to local regions. While highly effective, these models possess static architectures; once deployed, they expend the exact same computational energy on every single image, failing to capitalize on the fact that most visual queries are relatively simple.

\subsection{Compute Performance Tradeoff \& Elasticity}
To combat the memory overhead of storing multiple models for different power profiles (e.g., a "battery saver" model vs. a "high performance" model), elastic networks have gained prominence. The core concept of elasticity is training a single "supernet" from which multiple functional "subnetworks" can be extracted without retraining.

MatFormer \cite{kudugunta2024matformer} introduces a nested structure inside the standard Transformer block, allowing for the extraction of hundreds of accurate subnetworks. It achieves this by forcing the model to learn representations where the most critical information is packed into the foremost attention heads and neurons. Similarly, FlexiViT allows a single ViT to be trained to process a wide range of patch sizes, enabling flexible input resolutions at inference depending on the desired speed. BigNAS and MutualNet similarly explore elastic channel dimensions in CNNs. While MatFormer and FlexiViT allow a practitioner to select a model size based on a global compute budget, they are statically assigned per session or device. GEAR-ViT bridges this critical gap by migrating elasticity from a static, user-defined setting to a fully dynamic, per-image autonomous decision.

\subsection{Dynamic Neural Networks and Routing}
Dynamic neural networks seek to adapt their computational graph dynamically based on the input. Early work in this domain focused on Early Exiting (e.g., BranchyNet, EAViT), where an inference forward pass is halted early if an intermediate classifier reaches a predefined confidence threshold. However, early exiting often suffers from poorly calibrated intermediate representations in deep transformers.

Another rich line of work focuses on token pruning or token merging. DynamicViT introduces a prediction module to estimate the importance of each token, progressively pruning redundant tokens (like background patches) through the network layers. Token Merging (ToMe) bipartite-matches and merges similar tokens to reduce the sequence length dynamically. SparseViT applies sparse attention mechanisms to focus compute only on salient regions. 

Unlike token pruning methods that maintain the rigid channel and attention dimensions of the transformer blocks, GEAR-ViT targets the structural capacity of the network itself (MHA heads and FFN dimensions). By routing inputs based on overall scene complexity, our approach fundamentally alters the width of the network rather than the length of the token sequence. Notably, GEAR-ViT is highly complementary to token sparsification techniques; one could theoretically route an image to a smaller subnetwork \textit{and} prune its tokens, compounding the computational savings.

% 4. Methodology
\section{Methodology}

GEAR-ViT operates in two distinct phases: (1) Training the elastic ViT supernet via nested distillation, and (2) Training the entropy-guided subnetwork selection module to dynamically route inputs.

\subsection{Preliminaries: Standard ViT Architecture}
A standard Vision Transformer processes an image $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times C}$ by splitting it into a sequence of $N$ flattened 2D patches. These patches are linearly projected into $D$-dimensional embeddings, prepended with a learnable $[CLS]$ token, and added to position embeddings. The sequence is then passed through $L$ transformer blocks. Each block consists of Multi-Head Self-Attention (MSA) and a Feed-Forward Network (FFN).

For the MSA module with $H_{total}$ heads, the output is computed as:
\begin{equation}
    \text{MSA}(\mathbf{Z}) = \text{Concat}(\text{head}_1, ..., \text{head}_{H_{total}})\mathbf{W}^O
\end{equation}
where each $\text{head}_i = \text{Attention}(\mathbf{Z}\mathbf{W}^Q_i, \mathbf{Z}\mathbf{W}^K_i, \mathbf{Z}\mathbf{W}^V_i)$. The FFN consists of two linear layers separated by a GELU activation, expanding the hidden dimension $D$ by an expansion ratio $R_{total}$ (typically $R_{total}=4$):
\begin{equation}
    \text{FFN}(\mathbf{Z}) = \text{GELU}(\mathbf{Z}\mathbf{W}_1 + \mathbf{b}_1)\mathbf{W}_2 + \mathbf{b}_2
\end{equation}

\subsection{Stage 1: Flexible ViT Supernet Construction}
To ensure the network can dynamically scale to match the complexity of images, we train a ViT model in an elastic, nested manner. Instead of optimizing a single static graph, the supernet is trained such that smaller subnetworks are completely encapsulated within larger ones, following a Matryoshka-style representation learning protocol.

We introduce elasticity across two specific dimensions of the architecture: 
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Number of MHA Heads ($h$):} The number of active attention heads in each layer.
    \item \textbf{MLP Ratio ($r$):} The hidden dimension expansion ratio within the FFN.
\end{enumerate}

Let the configuration of the network be denoted as $C$:
\begin{equation}
    C = \left\{(r^l)^{L}_{l=1}, (h^l)^{L}_{l=1} \right\}
\end{equation}
Here, $L$ represents the total number of layers. In our implementation using a ViT-Base backbone ($L=12$), we define 4 discrete choices for the number of heads $h \in \{3, 6, 9, 12\}$ and 4 discrete choices for the MLP ratio $r \in \{1.0, 2.0, 3.0, 4.0\}$. 

To enforce the nested structure, we rearrange the weight matrices such that the most critical feature extractors are forced into the lowest indices. For a chosen subnetwork configuration with $h'$ heads and an $r'$ ratio, the truncated weight matrices are extracted simply by slicing:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\mathbf{W}}^Q = \mathbf{W}^Q_{[:, :h'\times d_h]}, \quad \hat{\mathbf{W}}_1 = \mathbf{W}_{1[:, :r'\times D]}
\end{equation}
where $d_h$ is the dimension per head. The supernet is trained jointly by sampling the largest full-capacity configuration, the smallest baseline configuration, and two randomly sampled intermediate configurations at each step, accumulating their losses. Because the smaller configurations strictly use a subset of the first $k$ heads and $m$ neurons of the full network, we successfully eliminate the need to store multiple discrete models.

\subsection{Stage 2: Entropy-Guided Routing}
Once the elastic model is fully trained, we freeze its weights to prepare for dynamic routing. The core innovation of GEAR-ViT is predicting the required model capacity before heavy computation begins. We calculate the information entropy of the raw image patches to derive a single scalar value representing scene complexity.

Given an image patch $\mathbf{p}_i$, we compute its grayscale histogram to estimate the probability distribution of pixel intensities $P(x)$. The Shannon entropy $E_i$ for the $i$-th patch is:
\begin{equation}
    E_i = - \sum_{x=0}^{255} P(x) \log_2 P(x)
\end{equation}
We then compute the mean patch entropy across all $N$ patches: $E_{mean} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N E_i$. This calculation is $O(N \cdot P_{size}^2)$ and takes less than $0.1$ms on a standard mobile GPU, representing negligible overhead.

A lightweight 2-layer MLP with a hidden dimension of 64 acts as our router. This router takes the mean patch entropy as its sole input, projecting it into logits that dictate the optimal subnetwork configuration for that specific image. 
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{z} = \text{GELU}(\mathbf{W}_{r2} \cdot \text{GELU}(\mathbf{W}_{r1}E_{mean} + \mathbf{b}_{r1}) + \mathbf{b}_{r2})
\end{equation}

Because selecting a discrete subnetwork architecture (an argmax operation over the available $h$ and $r$ configurations) is non-differentiable, we employ the Gumbel-Softmax trick to allow gradients to flow through the routing decisions during training. Let $\pi$ be the probability distribution over the 4 choices outputted by the router. We draw a sample $y$ using:
\begin{equation}
    y_i = \frac{\exp((\log(\pi_i) + g_i) / T)}{\sum_{j=1}^k \exp((\log(\pi_j) + g_j) / T)}
\end{equation}
where $g_i \sim \text{Gumbel}(0, 1)$ are i.i.d samples drawn from the Gumbel distribution, and $T$ is the temperature parameter. As $T \to 0$, the softmax computation smoothly approaches the discrete argmax function. During forward inference, we use the strict argmax (hard routing), while during the backward pass, we utilize the continuous gradients (straight-through estimator).

\subsection{Joint Optimization Objective}
The router must be optimized to balance two directly opposing forces: maximizing accuracy (which pushes the router to always select the full model) and minimizing compute (which pushes the router to always select the smallest subnetwork).

We freeze the ViT supernet and only update the router weights. The router is optimized using a joint loss function:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{p} =  \sum_{l=1}^{L} \left( \sum_{j=1}^{4} p_{mha}^{(l)}[j] \cdot C_{mha}[j] + \sum_{k=1}^{4} p_{mlp}^{(l)}[k] \cdot C_{mlp}[k] \right)
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}_{p}$ acts as a penalty term representing the expected parameter count or normalized GMACs of the chosen subnetwork at layer $l$. $p$ and $C$ are the Gumbel-Softmax probability and the static compute cost of a given configuration, respectively.

The performance preservation is enforced via Knowledge Distillation from the frozen full-capacity network. Let $\mathbf{s}_{full}$ be the output logits of the full model, and $\mathbf{s}_{sub}$ be the logits of the dynamically routed subnetwork. 
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{total} = \lambda \cdot \mathcal{L}_{p} + \beta \cdot \max(0, \mathcal{L}_{KL}(\mathbf{s}_{full} || \mathbf{s}_{sub}) - \tau)
\end{equation}
Here, $\mathcal{L}_{KL}$ represents the Kullback-Leibler divergence. $\lambda$ and $\beta$ are scaling coefficients to balance the loss magnitudes. $\tau$ is a crucial threshold hyperparameter: it establishes an acceptable "tolerance" for performance degradation. By implementing $\max(0, \mathcal{L}_{KL} - \tau)$, the router is heavily penalized only if the subnetwork deviates from the full model's predictions beyond the threshold $\tau$. If the subnetwork's predictions are within the tolerance, the KL penalty drops to zero, and the optimizer focuses entirely on minimizing the compute cost $\mathcal{L}_{p}$. 

\subsection{Alternative Router Input: The CLS Token}
During the architectural development of the routing module, we additionally experimented with using the $[CLS]$ token from the earliest layers of the ViT (e.g., Layer 1 or 2) as the condition for the router, acting as a learned, dense embedding of image complexity. 

While the $[CLS]$ token provides a rich semantic summary of the input space, it inherently requires a complete forward pass through the heavy initial layers of the largest subnetwork before a routing decision can even be calculated. By contrast, mean patch entropy is computed directly on the raw pixel space prior to any heavy matrix multiplications, introducing zero latency overhead to the transformer blocks. Empirical results showed that the raw entropy statistic was highly correlated with the $[CLS]$ token's complexity representation, yielding identical compute savings without the early-layer latency penalty.

\section{Experiments and Results}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
We use the standard ViT-Base-Patch16-224 model as the backbone for all experiments. The supernet is trained on ImageNet-1K using the AdamW optimizer for 300 epochs, with a base learning rate of $1e-3$, a cosine decay schedule, and a total batch size of 1024. We utilize standard data augmentation including Mixup, CutMix, and RandAugment. 

For the second phase (router training), the Gumbel-Softmax temperature $T$ is linearly annealed from $5.0$ down to $0.1$ over 50 epochs to smoothly transition from continuous exploration to discrete exploitation. The hyperparameters for the joint loss are set empirically to $\lambda = 0.1$ and $\beta = 1.0$, with the tolerance threshold initialized at $\tau = 0.05$. To robustly test the zero-shot and fine-tuned capabilities of the dynamically routed network, we scale our evaluation across standard computer vision domains including Image Classification, Cross-Modal Retrieval, Visual Question Answering (VQA), Object Detection, and Semantic Segmentation.

\subsection{Image Classification}

We evaluate GEAR-ViT across a diverse suite of image classification benchmarks including ImageNet-1K, CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, CUB-200, Oxford-IIIT Pets, and COCO. 

As shown in Table \ref{tab:main_results}, GEAR-ViT reduces the GMACs by up to \textbf{26.61\%} on CIFAR-10 and \textbf{11.58\%} on ImageNet-1K without any significant loss in top-1 accuracy compared to the full-capacity ViT. The variance in GMAC savings across datasets is a direct result of the entropy-guided routing. CIFAR-10 consists of low-resolution images with generally simpler, centralized subjects, leading to lower mean entropy scores. Consequently, the router aggressively selects smaller configurations. Conversely, ImageNet-1K contains higher variance in scene complexity, resulting in a more conservative overall compute reduction.

\textbf{Modulating the Tolerance Threshold ($\tau$):} 
We can explicitly control the accuracy-compute tradeoff curve post-training by regulating the hyperparameter $\tau$ in our joint loss. By raising the value of $\tau$, we intentionally relax the performance matching constraint between the full model and the selected subnetwork, allowing the router to aggressively prioritize computational savings over absolute accuracy. This provides extreme flexibility for deployment scenarios ranging from high-precision medical imaging to low-power background background monitoring.

\textbf{Implications for Mobile Edge Deployment:}
The theoretical GMAC reductions translate directly to tangible hardware benefits. For devices leveraging mobile GPUs (e.g., Adreno architectures) or dedicated NPUs via frameworks like OpenCL or NNAPI, static models enforce a uniform thermal load. GEAR-ViT's dynamic routing acts as an algorithmic thermal throttle. By processing simple queries (which often make up the bulk of user interactions in gallery searches) with 26\% fewer FLOPs, the system generates less heat, avoids hardware downclocking, and significantly extends battery life. Furthermore, because all $(4 \times 4)^{12}$ configurations share a contiguous block of weights in memory, the LPDDR RAM allocation remains strictly identical to a standard, static ViT-Base.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}
While GEAR-ViT provides substantial benefits, it is not without limitations. The reliance on image entropy as a proxy for complexity is generally robust, but it can be exploited. Images corrupted with artificial, high-frequency adversarial noise will produce artificially high mean patch entropy scores. In such adversarial scenarios, the router will misclassify the input as "highly complex" and unnecessarily default to the maximum capacity network. While this does not degrade accuracy, it negates the computational savings. Future work will explore incorporating learned, noise-resistant complexity estimators that maintain the ultra-low latency profile of the raw entropy calculation. Furthermore, while the GMAC reduction is theoretically massive, realizing precise 1:1 latency speedups on certain hardware accelerators requires specialized dynamic graph compilation support, which is currently an evolving feature in mobile deployment frameworks.

\section{Conclusion}
In this paper, we presented an entropy-guided subnetwork selection mechanism that dynamically scales the architecture of a Vision Transformer based on the intrinsic complexity of the input. During inference, simple images are processed rapidly with minimal Multiply-Accumulate operations, while complex images autonomously utilize the network's higher capacity. Empirical results across multiple benchmarks confirm that GEAR-ViT achieves a GMACs reduction of up to 26.61\% for classification tasks, 12.15\% for retrieval tasks, and substantial savings in dense prediction tasks, all with negligible degradation in accuracy. 

For real-world applications on edge devices, GEAR-ViT provides a scalable path forward. It can create a highly positive impact on latency-sensitive and battery-constrained features like Gallery Search, Image Tagger, Landmark Classifier, and broader on-device Multimodal solutions, moving the field away from rigid compute budgets toward intelligent, context-aware efficiency.
